### **Q1:** Analyse how physical, psychological, and sensory elements interact to influence overall customer satisfaction.

**Solution:**
Service design is a composite system. These elements do not operate in isolation; they are highly interdependent:
*   **Physical:** The tangible infrastructure (e.g., layout, equipment, facility footprint). If the physical layout creates bottlenecks (like long wait lines), it immediately degrades the psychological experience.
*   **Psychological:** The intangible perception of security, trust, and value. For example, a logically structured and secure system builds trust. If a service feels chaotic or unsafe, satisfaction plummets regardless of physical utility.
*   **Sensory:** The ambient conditions (lighting, noise, UI/UX aesthetics). Poor sensory inputs (e.g., excessive noise, cluttered interfaces) cause cognitive fatigue, which amplifies psychological frustration. 
**Conclusion:** A failure in the physical design creates negative sensory inputs, which ultimately destroys psychological trust. To maximize satisfaction, all three must be aligned to reduce friction.

### **Q3:** Estimate the break-even point quantity and analyse the effect of specific variable changes.

**Solution:**
First, establish the baseline metrics:
*   $\text{Sales Volume} = 25,000 \text{ units}$ 
*   $\text{Total Sales Value} = ₹4,00,000$ 
*   $\text{Selling Price (SP)} = \frac{4,00,000}{25,000} = ₹16 \text{ per unit}$
*   $\text{Fixed Cost (FC)} = ₹1,20,000$ 
*   $\text{Variable Cost (VC)} = ₹8 \text{ per unit}$

**(a) Break-Even Point (BEP) Quantity:**
$$BEP = \frac{\text{Fixed Cost}}{\text{Selling Price} - \text{Variable Cost}}$$
$$BEP = \frac{1,20,000}{16 - 8} = 15,000 \text{ units}$$

**(b) Scenario Analysis:**
*   **i. VC increases by 10%:** New VC = ₹8.80. New BEP = $\frac{1,20,000}{16 - 8.8} = 16,666.67 \text{ units}$. The break-even requirement increases.
*   **ii. SP decreases by 10%:** New SP = ₹14.40. New BEP = $\frac{1,20,000}{14.4 - 8} = 18,750 \text{ units}$. Profit margins shrink, requiring higher volume.
*   **iii. FC increases by ₹30,000:** New FC = ₹1,50,000. New BEP = $\frac{1,50,000}{16 - 8} = 18,750 \text{ units}$. Higher overhead forces higher minimum sales.

### **Q4:** Do you think that process layout is the best facility layout for a small clinic? Justify your answer.

**Solution:**
**Yes.** A process layout (functional layout) is objectively the correct choice here. 
*   **Reasoning:** In a clinic, the "product" (the patient) does not follow a standardized, linear sequence. Patient A might need a doctor and pharmacy, while Patient B needs an X-ray, lab work, and a doctor. 
*   A product layout (like an assembly line) would force patients through unnecessary steps, destroying throughput efficiency. A process layout groups similar resources (radiology in one area, pharmacy in another), allowing flexible, customized routing based on individual diagnostic requirements.

### **Q5:** Compute the cycle time per day and the minimum number of workstations.

**Solution:**
Given constraints:
*   $\text{Operating Time} = 8 \text{ hours/day} = 28,800 \text{ seconds/day}$
*   $\text{Daily Demand} = 400 \text{ units/day}$
*   $\text{Total Task Time } (\sum t) = 30 + 20 + 30 + 40 + 60 = 180 \text{ seconds}$

**Cycle Time (C):**
$$C = \frac{\text{Operating Time}}{\text{Daily Demand}} = \frac{28,800}{400} = 72 \text{ seconds/unit}$$

**Minimum Number of Workstations ($N_{min}$):**
$$N_{min} = \frac{\sum t}{C} = \frac{180}{72} = 2.5$$
*Since you cannot have a fraction of a workstation, round up.*
**Answer:** Minimum of **3** workstations.

### **Q6:** Analyse the case and suggest your advice to the procurement manager between a global and local supplier.

**Solution:**
Calculate the Total Landed Cost:
*   **Supplier Q (Global):** $\$9,000 + \$1,500 = \$10,500$
*   **Supplier R (Local):** $\$10,200 + \$300 = \$10,500$

**Advice:** The total cost is mathematically identical. Therefore, the decision must pivot to risk and operational efficiency. I strongly advise selecting **Supplier R (Local)**. While the base purchase price is higher, the local proximity significantly reduces lead times, mitigates international supply chain vulnerabilities (transit delays, geopolitical risk), and allows for faster communication and agile pivoting. Choose Q only if their component quality is demonstrably superior or if extreme volume scaling is required.

### **Q7:** Analyse the risks and benefits of continuing with global sourcing versus transitioning to local sourcing.

**Solution:**
*   **Global Sourcing:** 
    *   *Benefits:* Lower baseline material costs, access to massive economies of scale. 
    *   *Risks:* High vulnerability to transit disruptions, geopolitical friction, and longer lead times that paralyze inventory agility.
*   **Local Sourcing:**
    *   *Benefits:* Rapid delivery, high resilience against global shocks, easier quality auditing, and alignment with community pressure.
    *   *Risks:* Higher unit costs, potential capacity ceilings.
*   **Factors to Consider:** The firm must conduct a rigorous risk-adjusted cost analysis. Does the higher local unit cost offset the financial damage of a 3-week global shipping delay? They must evaluate supplier capacity, quality control, and the exact cost of potential downtime.

### **Q8:** Analyse the risks and benefits associated with the outsourcing decision taken by Apple Inc.

**Solution:**
*   **Benefits:** By outsourcing to massive manufacturers in China and India, Apple achieves unparalleled economies of scale and minimizes capital expenditure (they don't have to build or maintain the factories). This frees up capital to focus entirely on their core competencies: high-margin software ecosystems, R&D, and design.
*   **Risks:** Loss of direct oversight leads to severe supply chain vulnerabilities (as seen during global lockdowns), risk of intellectual property theft, and intense reputational damage tied to the labor practices of third-party contractors. 

### **Q9:** Analyse the statement "The purchasing strategy should support the business strategy" with an example.

**Solution:**
A supply chain exists to execute the overarching business objective; if they are misaligned, the firm bleeds cash. 
*   **Example:** Consider a company with a "Cost Leadership" strategy (e.g., Walmart). Their business goal is the lowest possible retail price. Therefore, their purchasing strategy must prioritize bulk volume discounts, aggressive vendor negotiations, and absolute logistical efficiency. 
*   Conversely, if a firm has a "Differentiation" strategy (e.g., a luxury brand), its purchasing strategy must prioritize the highest quality raw materials and exclusive supplier contracts, accepting higher costs to maintain brand prestige. A luxury brand utilizing Walmart's purchasing strategy would destroy its own product.

### **Q10:** Calculate the average inventory for a restaurant with an annual COGS of $520,000.

**Solution:**
Given parameters:
*   $\text{Annual COGS} = \$520,000$
*   $\text{Operating Weeks} = 52 \text{ weeks/year}$
*   $\text{Weeks of Supply} = 5 \text{ weeks}$

**1. Calculate Average Weekly COGS:**
$$\text{Average Weekly COGS} = \frac{\$520,000}{52} = \$10,000 \text{ per week}$$

**2. Calculate Average Inventory:**
$$\text{Average Inventory} = \text{Average Weekly COGS} \times \text{Weeks of Supply}$$
$$\text{Average Inventory} = \$10,000 \times 5 = \$50,000$$
