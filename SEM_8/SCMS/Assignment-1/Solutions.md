### **Question 1**
> "SCM encompasses all activities involved in the transformation of goods from the raw material stage to the final stage, and ultimately reaching the end customer." Analyse this statement with the help of a suitable diagram. 

**Analysis:**
Supply Chain Management (SCM) is not a single function; it is the end-to-end network of entities, processes, and resources required to convert basic materials into finished products delivered to the end-user. The statement accurately reflects that SCM bridges multiple functional boundaries—procurement, manufacturing, logistics, and retail—ensuring a continuous flow of physical goods, information, and capital.

**Text-Based Diagram of the Supply Chain Flow:**
```text
[Upstream / Supply Network] 
   1. Raw Material Extraction (e.g., mining, farming)
        ↓
   2. Tier-2 / Tier-1 Suppliers (component processing)
        ↓
[Focal Firm / Internal SCM]
   3. Manufacturer (assembly/production)
        ↓
[Downstream / Distribution Network]
   4. Distributor / Wholesaler (bulk storage)
        ↓
   5. Retailer (point of sale)
        ↓
   6. End Customer (consumption)
```
*Note: The flow of goods goes top to bottom, while the flow of demand and information goes bottom to top.*

### **Question 4**
> "Is a commodity position always bad?". Analyse using the competitive advantage matrix. 

**Analysis:**
No, a commodity position is not fundamentally bad. In the competitive advantage matrix (which maps competitive advantage based on differentiation versus cost leadership), a commodity position represents low product differentiation. Products are essentially identical across competitors (e.g., steel, basic chemicals, agricultural goods). 

Firms in a commodity position can still achieve massive profitability and a strong competitive advantage if they aggressively pursue **Cost Leadership**. By optimizing supply chain efficiencies, achieving economies of scale, and minimizing operational waste, a firm can maintain the lowest cost structure in the market. In a commodity market, price is the only differentiator; therefore, the lowest-cost producer wins.

### **Question 5**
> "COVID-19 pandemic witnessed disruptions in the supply chain management of all most all commodities in India". Analyse which of the two effects, viz., Ratchet or Ripple better explains this disturbance in SCM. 

**Analysis:**
The **Ripple Effect** explains this disturbance. The ripple effect in SCM occurs when a disruption at one node (e.g., factory shutdowns due to lockdowns) propagates downstream and upstream, impacting the performance and stability of the entire network. COVID-19 caused structural disruptions (loss of labor, closed borders, raw material shortages) that cascaded through the supply chain. 

The Ratchet Effect is irrelevant here; it is an economic concept describing the inability of prices, wages, or systems to easily reverse downward once they have been elevated.

### **Question 6**
> A warehouse introduces barcode scanning to reduce manual errors. Analyse the likely impact of introduction of barcode on productivity in the supply chain. 

**Analysis:**
Introducing barcode scanning will significantly increase labor productivity and process velocity. 
1. **Time Efficiency:** Data capture changes from minutes of manual entry to milliseconds of scanning, increasing the volume of units processed per labor hour.
2. **Error Reduction:** Manual entry has an inherent error rate (approx. 1 in 300 keystrokes). Barcodes reduce this to near zero, eliminating the operational downtime and rework costs associated with correcting shipping/receiving mistakes.
3. **Inventory Accuracy:** Real-time visibility improves forecasting and reduces safety stock requirements, tightening the entire supply chain footprint.

### **Question 7**
> Logistics refers to "planning, implementing, and controlling the physical flows of materials and finished goods from point of origin to point of use to meet the customer's need at a profit". Analyse this statement in the context of relationship between logistics and supply chain management. 

**Analysis:**
The statement highlights that logistics is a **subset** of Supply Chain Management. SCM is the overarching framework that dictates strategy, supplier relationships, product development, and overall network design. Logistics is the execution arm of that strategy. While SCM decides *who* to buy from and *where* to locate a factory, logistics manages the literal, physical reality of moving those materials efficiently (via trucking, warehousing, and inventory tracking) from point A to point B.

### **Question 8**
> Delta Pizzaria is a local pizza shop in Bhubaneswar which operates 10 hours a day with the help of 20 workers. Each day, it can be able to produce and sale 500 pizzas. It is estimated that on an average 2 pizzas has been produced in each labour hour by all the pizza shops in the city. Analyse, whether Delta Pizzaria enjoys competitive advantage in the production of pizza, measured through its labour productivity. 

**Analysis:**
First, calculate Delta Pizzaria's labor productivity. 

$$\text{Total Labor Hours} = 20 \text{ workers} \times 10 \text{ hours/day} = 200 \text{ hours/day}$$
$$\text{Labor Productivity} = \frac{\text{Total Output}}{\text{Total Labor Hours}} = \frac{500}{200} = 2.5 \text{ pizzas/hour}$$

The city average is 2 pizzas per labor hour. Because Delta Pizzaria produces 2.5 pizzas per labor hour, it possesses a measurable operational efficiency over its competitors. **Yes**, Delta Pizzaria enjoys a competitive advantage in labor productivity.

### **Question 9**
> A supply chain facility produces 5,000 units using the following inputs: (a) Labor: 400 hours at ₹200/hour (b) Machine: 300 hours at ₹150/hour (c) Energy cost: ₹20,000. Next month, output increases to 6,000 units with (a) Labor: 420 hours at ₹200/hour (b) Machine: 320 hours at ₹150/hour (c) Energy cost: ₹22,000. Analyze the change in total factor productivity (TFP). 

**Analysis:**
Total Factor Productivity (TFP) measures output relative to the combined cost of all inputs.

**Month 1:**
* Output = 5,000 units
* Input Costs:
    * Labor: $400 \times 200 = ₹80,000$
    * Machine: $300 \times 150 = ₹45,000$
    * Energy: $₹20,000$
    * Total Input Cost = $80,000 + 45,000 + 20,000 = ₹145,000$
* TFP (Month 1) = $\frac{5000}{145000} \approx 0.03448 \text{ units/₹}$

**Month 2:**
* Output = 6,000 units
* Input Costs:
    * Labor: $420 \times 200 = ₹84,000$
    * Machine: $320 \times 150 = ₹48,000$
    * Energy: $₹22,000$
    * Total Input Cost = $84,000 + 48,000 + 22,000 = ₹154,000$
* TFP (Month 2) = $\frac{6000}{154000} \approx 0.03896 \text{ units/₹}$

**Change:**
$$\text{Percentage Change} = \left( \frac{0.03896 - 0.03448}{0.03448} \right) \times 100 \approx 13\% \text{ increase}$$

The facility achieved roughly a 13% increase in total factor productivity, indicating economies of scale or improved operational efficiency during the expansion.

### **Question 10**
> A supply chain facility increases output from 1500 units to 1800 units, while labor hours increase from 300 to 320 hours. Analyze the change in labour productivity. 

**Analysis:**
**Initial State:**
$$\text{Labor Productivity}_1 = \frac{1500 \text{ units}}{300 \text{ hours}} = 5 \text{ units/hour}$$

**New State:**
$$\text{Labor Productivity}_2 = \frac{1800 \text{ units}}{320 \text{ hours}} = 5.625 \text{ units/hour}$$

**Change:**
$$\text{Percentage Change} = \left( \frac{5.625 - 5}{5} \right) \times 100 = 12.5\% \text{ increase}$$

Labor productivity improved by 12.5%. The facility successfully scaled its output at a faster rate than its labor inputs, signifying better utilization of the workforce.
