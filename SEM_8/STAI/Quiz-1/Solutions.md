### **Q1: Karma is the cause of diversity: explain.**
**A:** In Indian philosophy, the law of Karma dictates that every action yields a proportional reaction. The diversity in human conditions and inequalities is not random chance. It is the direct consequence of the accumulated actions (karmas) and resulting psychological tendencies (samskaras) of individuals across past and present lives.

### **Q2: Name and explain 5 kosas in panchakosa system.**
**A:** The Taittiriya Upanishad categorizes human existence into five sheaths (kosas):
* **Annamaya Kosa:** The physical body, built and sustained by food.
* **Pranamaya Kosa:** The vital energy or breath (prana) driving biological functions.
* **Manomaya Kosa:** The mental layer, responsible for sensory processing and basic thought.
* **Vijnanamaya Kosa:** The intellect sheath, encompassing logic and higher reasoning.
* **Anandamaya Kosa:** The bliss sheath, the innermost causal body characterized by peace.

### **Q3: Modern science and technology have played the most important role in bringing people so close to each other. Justify this.**
**A:** Modern technology eliminates spatial and temporal barriers. Advancements in telecommunications and the internet enable instantaneous global communication. Concurrently, modern aviation and high-speed transit physically compress geography, resulting in a highly interconnected global network where geographic isolation is practically obsolete.

### **Q4: Explain how, on earth, different philosophies got developed locally, yet each one claims itself to be universal.**
**A:** Philosophies originate as local frameworks to solve specific environmental or cultural problems. Because the fundamental human condition—mortality, suffering, and the search for meaning—is identical everywhere, thinkers abstract their localized solutions into generalized principles. They claim universality because they address universal human constants, despite their localized origins.

### **Q5: List the five mahavakyas from different Upanishads.**

### **Q6: Vedic Hindus evinced special interest in Geometry and Astronomy. State the reasons for above.**
**A:** 
* **Geometry:** Strictly required for the precise construction of fire altars (Yajnas). The Sulba Sutras dictate exact shapes and areas necessary for rituals to be considered geometrically valid.
* **Astronomy:** Required to calculate precise auspicious times, lunar phases, and planetary alignments to schedule rituals accurately, and to track seasons for an agrarian society.

### **Q8: Explain the major contributions of Charaka and Sushruta, to medical science, in the ancient India.**
**A:** 
* **Charaka:** Foundational figure of internal medicine (Ayurveda). Authored the *Charaka Samhita*, formalizing concepts of digestion, metabolism, the three doshas, and emphasizing disease prevention.
* **Sushruta:** Pioneer of ancient surgery. Authored the *Sushruta Samhita*, detailing over 300 surgical procedures (including reconstructive rhinoplasty) and classifying over 100 surgical instruments.

### **Q9: Describe the Architecture during the Harappan period.**
**A:** Harappan architecture was utilitarian and defined by rigorous urban planning. Features included grid-patterned streets intersecting at right angles, standardized baked-brick construction, multi-story residential buildings, and an advanced, covered underground drainage network. Key public structures included massive granaries and the Great Bath.

### **Q10: Name the 4 Vedas. Which of them is mainly about science?**
**A:** The four Vedas are the Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, and Atharvaveda. The **Atharvaveda** is the closest to applied science, departing from strict ritualism to address practical knowledge like early medicine, anatomy, and agricultural practices.
