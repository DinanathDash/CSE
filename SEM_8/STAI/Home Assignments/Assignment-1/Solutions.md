### **1. Why is it necessary to address the dharma paradigm using scientific language?**
**Answer:** To bridge ancient philosophical frameworks with modern empirical understanding. Translating traditional concepts into scientific language makes them accessible, logically verifiable, and relevant to contemporary academic and global audiences.

### **2. What were the main features of architecture during the Harappan period?**
**Answer:** Harappan architecture was defined by rigorous urban planning. Key features included grid-patterned streets, standardized burnt brick construction, highly advanced underground drainage systems, centralized citadels, granaries, and the Great Bath. 

### **3. How was architecture in the Vedic period different from Harappan architecture?**
**Answer:** Harappan architecture was heavily urbanized and permanent (brick and stone). Vedic architecture transitioned to rural, agrarian settlements using perishable materials like wood, bamboo, and thatch. The primary architectural focus shifted from city planning to the precise geometric construction of sacrificial altars (Yajnasalas) dictated by the Sulba Sutras.

### **4. Briefly explain the primary cause of natural diversity according to Darwin's theory versus Buddhist philosophy.**
**Answer:** 
* **Darwin's Theory:** Natural diversity is driven by random genetic mutations and natural selection (survival of the fittest) adapting to environmental pressures over time.
* **Buddhist Philosophy:** Diversity is driven by *Karma*—the accumulated intentional actions, desires, and moral choices of beings, which dictate the conditions of their rebirth and existence.

### **5. What is the "encephalization factor," and which being possesses the highest value?**
**Answer:** The Encephalization Quotient (EQ) is a biological metric used to measure relative brain size compared to the expected brain size for an animal's body mass. It serves as a rough estimate of intelligence. Humans possess the highest EQ value.

### **6. What are Buddhist stupas and viharas?**
**Answer:** 
* **Stupas:** Dome-shaped monumental structures designed to house sacred relics of the Buddha or esteemed monks. 
* **Viharas:** Residential quarters or monasteries built for Buddhist monks to live, study, and meditate.

### **7. What are the main styles of temple architecture in India?**
**Answer:** There are three distinct classical styles:
* **Nagara:** Northern style, characterized by a beehive-shaped, curving tower (Shikhara).
* **Dravida:** Southern style, characterized by a stepped, pyramid-shaped tower (Vimana) and large gateway structures (Gopurams).
* **Vesara:** A hybrid style prevalent in the Deccan region that combines elements of both Nagara and Dravida.

### **8. What is rock-cut architecture?**
**Answer:** The practice of creating structures, temples, and sculptures by excavating solid rock where it naturally occurs, rather than building up from individual masonry units. The Ajanta and Ellora caves are prime examples.

### **9. List the core assumptions found in Darwin's theory of evolution.**
**Answer:** 
* Variation naturally exists within populations.
* Traits are heritable and passed from parents to offspring.
* Organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support, leading to competition.
* Natural selection favors traits that provide a survival and reproductive advantage.

### **10. What is the scope of Ayurveda in ancient India?**
**Answer:** Ayurveda is a holistic medical system. Its scope is categorized into eight branches (Ashtanga Ayurveda), which include internal medicine (Kaya Chikitsa), surgery (Shalya Tantra), pediatrics, toxicology, geriatrics, and reproductive health. 

### **11. What is the origin and antiquity of Ayurveda?**
**Answer:** Ayurveda is traditionally considered an *Upaveda* (supplementary text) of the Atharva Veda. Its origins date back to the Vedic period (c. 1500–1000 BCE), with its core classical texts (Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita) codified around 200 BCE to 400 CE.

### **12. What is the fundamental difference between simply surviving nature and surmounting natural hindrances?**
**Answer:** Surviving means operating strictly within biological and environmental limits (passive adaptation). Surmounting hindrances requires conscious effort, intellect, and technology to modify the environment, conquer natural limitations, and improve living conditions.

### **13. How did Ayurveda develop and decline over time?**
**Answer:** It developed rapidly during the classical era through rigorous empirical observation, advancing notably in surgery and herbal medicine. It declined during the medieval period due to foreign invasions, a loss of royal patronage, the introduction of Unani medicine, and later, the dominance of Western (Allopathic) medicine under British rule.

### **14. How was Ayurveda applied to other forms of life?**
**Answer:** Ancient Indians applied Ayurvedic principles to non-human life forms, creating specialized branches like *Ashvayurveda* (veterinary medicine for horses), *Hastyayurveda* (for elephants), and *Vrikshayurveda* (botany and plant health).

### **15. How did Ayurveda spread outside India?**
**Answer:** It spread via ancient trade routes (like the Silk Road), the movement of Buddhist monks across Asia, and the translation of foundational Sanskrit texts into Arabic, Persian, Tibetan, and Chinese.

### **16. How is "Karma" defined specifically within the Rajavartika of the Jaina tradition?**
**Answer:** In Jainism, Karma is not just a philosophical concept but a physical reality. It is defined as a subtle, imperceptible material matter (particles) that attaches to the soul (jiva) as a result of one's actions and passions, obscuring the soul's natural purity.

### **17. What was astronomy called in the Brahmana period?**
**Answer:** It was referred to as *Nakshatra Vidya* (the science of asterisms/stars) or *Jyotisha*.

### **18. How do long-lasting desire and effort (Karma) contribute to the perceived superiority of humans among species?**
**Answer:** Humans have the capacity for *Purushartha* (deliberate, conscious effort) and moral choice. Unlike animals, which are driven by basic instinct, humans can harness long-lasting desire and effort to shape their destiny, conquer their environment, and ultimately seek intellectual or spiritual liberation.

### **19. How was the earth described in the Satapatha Brahmana?**
**Answer:** The *Satapatha Brahmana* often describes the earth conceptually and ritualistically, recognizing its spherical nature (Parimandala) while primarily treating it as the foundational realm connected intricately to the atmosphere and heavens.

### **20. Who is said to have calculated solar eclipses in the Rg-Veda?**
**Answer:** The sage Atri and his descendants are credited with observing and understanding the mechanics of solar eclipses, metaphorically described in the texts as "rescuing the sun from the demon Svarbhanu."
