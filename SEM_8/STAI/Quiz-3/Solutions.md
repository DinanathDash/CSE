### **Q1. Explain the sentence "no deity is purely divine or purely phenomenal in its manifestation". As stated in Rigveda (PP 72 last)**
**Answer:** In Vedic philosophy, deities (Devas) are bridging concepts. They are not just abstract spiritual entities (purely divine), nor are they just mindless natural forces (purely phenomenal). A deity like Surya represents both the physical sun you see in the sky and the underlying cosmic intelligence or divine principle that governs its existence and movement.

### **Q2. Explain the scope & power of 'Agni'. (PP 77-80)**
**Answer:** Agni (Fire) is central to Vedic thought due to its transformative power. Its scope covers three realms: terrestrial fire (combustion/ritual fire), atmospheric fire (lightning), and celestial fire (the sun). Its primary power is as a mediator or messenger; in rituals, Agni transforms physical oblations into subtle energy to reach the gods, symbolizing the purification and upward movement of human consciousness.

### **Q3. Name five basic elements in the universe. Why Vedic seers opine that any change in natural balance in them causes a lot of problems.**
**Answer:** The five basic elements (Pancha Mahabhutas) are Earth (Prithvi), Water (Jala), Fire (Agni), Air (Vayu), and Space/Ether (Akasha). Vedic seers viewed the universe (macrocosm) and the human body (microcosm) as interconnected systems composed of these identical elements. A disruption in the macrocosmic balance (nature) inevitably triggers a breakdown in the microcosmic balance (human health and society), leading to disease, natural disasters, and chaos.

### **Q4. How many types of wind exist in human body? Name them. (PP 188)**
**Answer:** There are five primary types of wind (Vayus or Pranas) that govern physiological functions. They are:
1. **Prana:** Inward/forward movement (respiration).
2. **Apana:** Downward/outward movement (excretion/reproduction).
3. **Samana:** Equalizing movement (digestion/assimilation).
4. **Udana:** Upward movement (speech/swallowing).
5. **Vyana:** Diffusive movement (circulation throughout the body).

### **Q5. Science was used as a tool of empire building by the European colonialist. Justify.**
**Answer:** The British did not introduce science to India for intellectual enlightenment; they used it for extraction. Botany and geology were utilized to map and exploit natural resources. Cartography and surveying were used to define and control territory. Technologies like the railways and telegraphs were built primarily to move troops quickly and export raw materials out of the country, solidifying imperial control.

### **Q6. Give an account on the association of native Indian in the early scientific endeavor.**
**Answer:** Initially, native Indians were deliberately kept out of primary research and relegated to subordinate roles like field assistants, clerks, and low-level surveyors to serve colonial economic interests. It wasn't until the late 19th and early 20th centuries that figures like J.C. Bose, P.C. Ray, and Mahendralal Sircar pushed back by establishing independent, native-led institutions (like the IACS) to conduct autonomous basic research.

### **Q7. Discuss the phase in which Indian natives were introduced to modern sciences by the colonial masters.**
**Answer:** This occurred primarily in the mid-19th century with the establishment of medical colleges, engineering schools (like Roorkee), and universities (1857). The introduction was strictly utilitarian. The colonial administration needed cheap, locally trained subordinate staff—draftsmen, apothecaries, and engineers—to maintain public works, run the railways, and assist in surveys, not to foster native scientific leadership.

### **Q8. How the modern science helped the Nation building in independent India? Discuss.**
**Answer:** Post-1947, science was positioned as the primary engine for modernization and poverty alleviation. It enabled massive infrastructure projects (dams and power plants for industrialization), the Green Revolution (using high-yield seeds and fertilizers to achieve food security and prevent famine), and the establishment of elite institutions (IITs) to create a self-reliant, technically skilled workforce.

### **Q9. Discuss the role of CSIR for the development of modern India in the sphere of R&D.**
**Answer:** The Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) was critical in bridging the gap between academic science and industrial application. By establishing a vast network of national laboratories, CSIR focused on applied research in chemicals, drugs, aerospace, and metallurgy. This reduced independent India's reliance on imported technology and built indigenous industrial capacity.

### **Q10. Give an account of independent India space research program under the aegis of ISRO.**
**Answer:** Unlike the US or USSR, ISRO did not start with a focus on geopolitical prestige or manned missions. Founded under Vikram Sarabhai, its strict mandate was socio-economic application. It focused on developing satellite technology (INSAT, IRS) for practical needs: telecommunications, weather forecasting, disaster management, and resource mapping, before successfully expanding into cost-effective planetary exploration (Chandrayaan) and commercial launches.
